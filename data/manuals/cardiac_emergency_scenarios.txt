1. CARDIAC ARREST SCENARIOS
================================================================================

SCENARIO 1A: WITNESSED ADULT CARDIAC ARREST
Location: Public place (mall, office, home)
Presentation:
- Sudden collapse
- Unresponsive to verbal/physical stimuli
- No normal breathing (agonal breathing doesn't count)
- No pulse palpable

Immediate Actions (Chain of Survival):
1. Recognition and activation of EMS (call 911/emergency services)
2. Early CPR with chest compressions
3. Rapid defibrillation with AED
4. Advanced life support
5. Post-cardiac arrest care

CPR Protocol (Adult):
- Hand position: Lower half of breastbone
- Compression depth: 2-2.4 inches (5-6 cm)
- Compression rate: 100-120 per minute
- Allow complete chest recoil
- Minimize interruptions
- 30:2 compression to ventilation ratio

SCENARIO 1B: UNWITNESSED CARDIAC ARREST
Considerations:
- Unknown down time
- Possible hypothermia
- Potential trauma as cause
- Medication overdose possibility

Modified Approach:
1. Assess for signs of life
2. Check for obvious injuries
3. Consider cervical spine protection
4. Begin CPR if no pulse
5. Continue until EMS arrival or ROSC

SCENARIO 1C: DROWNING CARDIAC ARREST
Special Considerations:
- Hypothermia common
- Aspiration likely
- Electrolyte imbalances
- Prolonged submersion effects

Protocol Modifications:
1. Remove from water safely
2. Begin rescue breathing immediately
3. Start chest compressions on firm surface
4. Warm the patient during resuscitation
5. Continue longer than usual (hypothermic protection)

SCENARIO 1D: DRUG OVERDOSE CARDIAC ARREST
Common Causes:
- Opioid overdose
- Stimulant toxicity
- Tricyclic antidepressant poisoning
- Multiple drug interactions

Special Interventions:
1. Administer naloxone if opioid suspected
2. Consider sodium bicarbonate for TCA overdose
3. Avoid certain medications (procainamide with cocaine)
4. Extended resuscitation efforts
5. Toxicology consultation

2. ACUTE MYOCARDIAL INFARCTION (HEART ATTACK)
================================================================================

SCENARIO 2A: CLASSIC STEMI PRESENTATION
Signs and Symptoms:
- Severe crushing chest pain
- Pain radiating to left arm, jaw, back
- Diaphoresis (sweating)
- Nausea and vomiting
- Shortness of breath

Assessment:
1. OPQRST pain assessment
2. Vital signs including oxygen saturation
3. 12-lead ECG within 10 minutes
4. Cardiac biomarkers
5. Chest X-ray

Immediate Treatment:
1. Oxygen if SpO2 <90%
2. Aspirin 325mg chewed (if not allergic)
3. Nitroglycerin sublingual (if BP >90 systolic)
4. Morphine for severe pain
5. Beta-blocker if stable

SCENARIO 2B: SILENT MI (DIABETIC/ELDERLY)
Atypical Presentations:
- Vague chest discomfort
- Shortness of breath only
- Fatigue
- Nausea
- Confusion (elderly)

High-Risk Populations:
- Diabetes mellitus
- Elderly (>75 years)
- Women
- Chronic kidney disease
- Previous MI

Assessment Strategy:
1. High index of suspicion
2. Serial ECGs
3. Troponin levels
4. Echocardiogram
5. Risk stratification tools

SCENARIO 2C: NSTEMI PRESENTATION
Characteristics:
- Chest discomfort at rest
- No ST elevation on ECG
- Positive cardiac biomarkers
- Often crescendo pattern

Management:
1. Dual antiplatelet therapy
2. Anticoagulation
3. Risk stratification
4. Early invasive strategy if high risk
5. Medical optimization

SCENARIO 2D: UNSTABLE ANGINA
Features:
- New onset severe angina
- Crescendo pattern
- Rest angina
- Post-MI angina
- Normal biomarkers initially

Treatment Approach:
1. Aggressive medical therapy
2. Risk stratification
3. Early catheterization if high risk
4. Medical optimization
5. Lifestyle counseling

3. ARRHYTHMIA SCENARIOS
================================================================================

SCENARIO 3A: VENTRICULAR FIBRILLATION
Presentation:
- Cardiac arrest
- Irregular, chaotic rhythm
- No effective cardiac output
- Requires immediate defibrillation

Treatment:
1. Immediate defibrillation (200J biphasic)
2. Resume CPR immediately for 2 minutes
3. Check rhythm and repeat shock if indicated
4. Epinephrine 1mg IV every 3-5 minutes
5. Consider amiodarone 300mg IV

SCENARIO 3B: VENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA
Stable VT:
- Pulse present
- Blood pressure maintained
- Conscious

Treatment:
1. IV access and oxygen
2. Amiodarone 150mg IV over 10 minutes
3. Procainamide alternative
4. Prepare for cardioversion
5. Treat underlying causes

Unstable VT:
- Hypotension
- Altered mental status
- Chest pain
- Heart failure

Treatment:
1. Immediate synchronized cardioversion
2. Sedate if conscious
3. 100J initial energy
4. Increase energy if unsuccessful
5. Post-conversion monitoring

SCENARIO 3C: SUPRAVENTRICULAR TACHYCARDIA
Presentation:
- Heart rate >150 bpm
- Narrow QRS complex
- Regular rhythm
- Sudden onset/termination

Vagal Maneuvers:
1. Valsalva maneuver (bear down)
2. Carotid massage (if no bruits)
3. Ice water face immersion
4. Leg elevation with head down

Pharmacological:
1. Adenosine 6mg IV rapid push
2. If no response, 12mg IV
3. Follow with rapid saline flush
4. Monitor for conversion

SCENARIO 3D: ATRIAL FIBRILLATION WITH RVR
Acute Onset (<48 hours):
- Cardioversion consideration
- Anticoagulation if risk factors
- Rate vs rhythm control

Chronic AFib:
- Rate control priority
- Long-term anticoagulation
- Rhythm control in selected patients

Rate Control Agents:
1. Metoprolol 25-50mg PO BID
2. Diltiazem 120-360mg daily
3. Digoxin 0.125-0.25mg daily
4. Avoid in WPW syndrome

4. CARDIOGENIC SHOCK
================================================================================

SCENARIO 4A: ACUTE MI WITH CARDIOGENIC SHOCK
Hemodynamic Profile:
- Cardiac index <2.2 L/min/mÂ²
- PCWP >15 mmHg
- Systolic BP <90 mmHg
- Signs of organ hypoperfusion

Immediate Management:
1. Intraaortic balloon pump
2. Vasopressor support (norepinephrine)
3. Inotropic support (dobutamine)
4. Emergency revascularization
5. Mechanical circulatory support consideration

SCENARIO 4B: ACUTE HEART FAILURE WITH SHOCK
Precipitating Factors:
- Medication noncompliance
- Dietary indiscretion
- Arrhythmias
- Infection
- Myocardial ischemia

Treatment Strategy:
1. IV diuretics (furosemide)
2. Vasodilators if normotensive
3. Inotropes if low cardiac output
4. BiPAP for respiratory support
5. Treat precipitating factors

SCENARIO 4C: RIGHT HEART FAILURE SHOCK
Causes:
- Massive pulmonary embolism
- Right ventricular infarction
- Pulmonary hypertension crisis
- Tricuspid valve disease

Management:
1. Volume optimization
2. Pulmonary vasodilators
3. Inotropic support
4. Avoid excessive preload reduction
5. Treat underlying cause

5. SPECIAL CARDIAC SITUATIONS
================================================================================

SCENARIO 5A: CARDIAC TAMPONADE
Beck's Triad:
- Elevated JVP
- Hypotension
- Muffled heart sounds

Additional Signs:
- Pulsus paradoxus >20 mmHg
- Tachycardia
- Distant heart sounds

Emergency Treatment:
1. Immediate pericardiocentesis
2. Volume expansion
3. Inotropic support
4. Avoid diuretics
5. Surgical drainage

SCENARIO 5B: ACUTE AORTIC DISSECTION
Type A (Ascending):
- Chest pain radiating to back
- Pulse deficits
- Aortic regurgitation
- Neurological symptoms

Type B (Descending):
- Back pain
- Hypertension
- Renal insufficiency
- Limb ischemia

Management:
1. Blood pressure control (beta-blockers first)
2. Pain control
3. Emergency surgery for Type A
4. Medical management for uncomplicated Type B
5. Serial imaging

SCENARIO 5C: PULMONARY EMBOLISM
Massive PE:
- Hemodynamic instability
- RV strain on echo
- Elevated troponins
- Hypoxemia

Treatment:
1. Anticoagulation (heparin)
2. Thrombolytic therapy if massive
3. Embolectomy consideration
4. IVC filter if anticoagulation contraindicated
5. Supportive care

SCENARIO 5D: HYPERTENSIVE EMERGENCY
Malignant Hypertension:
- BP >180/120 with end-organ damage
- Papilledema
- Acute kidney injury
- Microangiopathic hemolytic anemia

Treatment:
1. Reduce BP by 10-20% in first hour
2. Nicardipine or clevidipine IV
3. Avoid sublingual nifedipine
4. Monitor for organ function
5. Gradual BP reduction over 24-48 hours

6. PEDIATRIC CARDIAC EMERGENCIES
================================================================================

SCENARIO 6A: PEDIATRIC CARDIAC ARREST
Differences from Adult:
- Respiratory causes more common
- Smaller blood volume
- Different drug dosing
- Size-appropriate equipment

BLS Modifications:
- Infant: 2 thumb technique
- Child: Heel of one hand
- Compression depth: 1/3 chest depth
- Rate: 100-120/minute
- Ratio: 30:2 (one rescuer), 15:2 (two rescuer)

SCENARIO 6B: CONGENITAL HEART DISEASE EMERGENCY
Cyanotic Episodes:
- Tetralogy of Fallot
- Tricuspid atresia
- Pulmonary atresia

Management:
1. Knee-chest position
2. Oxygen administration
3. Morphine for agitation
4. Volume expansion
5. Beta-blocker for spells

SCENARIO 6C: KAWASAKI DISEASE CARDIAC COMPLICATIONS
Coronary Artery Aneurysms:
- Fever >5 days
- Conjunctivitis
- Rash
- Lymphadenopathy
- Oral changes

Cardiac Management:
1. Echocardiography
2. Aspirin therapy
3. IVIG if acute phase
4. Anticoagulation if large aneurysms
5. Long-term cardiology follow-up

7. ADVANCED CARDIAC LIFE SUPPORT
================================================================================

ACLS ALGORITHMS

VF/Pulseless VT:
1. CPR + shock
2. Epinephrine 1mg q3-5min
3. Amiodarone 300mg, then 150mg
4. Consider reversible causes

Asystole/PEA:
1. High-quality CPR
2. Epinephrine 1mg q3-5min
3. Consider atropine 1mg (asystole)
4. Treat reversible causes

Bradycardia:
1. Atropine 0.5mg IV
2. Transcutaneous pacing
3. Dopamine 2-10 mcg/kg/min
4. Epinephrine 2-10 mcg/min

Tachycardia:
1. Vagal maneuvers
2. Adenosine 6mg, then 12mg
3. Cardioversion if unstable
4. Rate control if stable

H's and T's (Reversible Causes):
- Hypovolemia
- Hypoxia
- Hydrogen ion (acidosis)
- Hypo/hyperkalemia
- Hypothermia
- Toxins
- Tamponade
- Tension pneumothorax
- Thrombosis (pulmonary)
- Thrombosis (coronary)

8. POST-RESUSCITATION CARE
================================================================================

ROSC MANAGEMENT
Initial Stabilization:
1. Airway management
2. Oxygenation (SpO2 94-98%)
3. Ventilation (ETCO2 35-40 mmHg)
4. Blood pressure support
5. Temperature management

Targeted Temperature Management:
- Target 32-36Â°C
- Initiate within 6 hours
- Maintain for 24 hours
- Gradual rewarming
- Monitor for complications

Neurological Assessment:
1. Glasgow Coma Scale
2. Pupillary response
3. Brainstem reflexes
4. Motor responses
5. EEG monitoring

Prognostication:
- Avoid early withdrawal
- Multimodal assessment
- Wait 72 hours minimum
- Consider confounders
- Family communication

Long-term Care:
1. Cardiac catheterization
2. Device therapy evaluation
3. Heart failure management
4. Rehabilitation planning
5. Family support
